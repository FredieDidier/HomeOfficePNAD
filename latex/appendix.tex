\section{The PNADC and the advanced panel identification}\label{app:pnadc}

\paragraph{The survey.} The Continuous National Household Sample Survey (\emph{Pesquisa Nacional por Amostra de Domicílios Cont\'inua}, PNADC) is the household labor-force survey conducted by the Brazilian Institute of Geography and Statistics (IBGE). It is a stratified, multi-stage probability sample drawn from a master sample of census tracts and is the source of Brazil's official labor-market statistics: each quarter it covers on the order of 200,000 households across roughly 16,000 census tracts, with interviewing spread over the 12 weeks of the quarter. Households enter under a rotating panel scheme and are interviewed in five consecutive quarters before leaving the sample, so a given household, and the individuals in it, can be observed up to five times over about 15 months, the short longitudinal dimension this paper exploits. Because the sample is not self-weighting, all statistics and estimates use the survey's calibrated sampling weights. The survey has been fielded quarterly since the first quarter of 2012; the main outcome, the location at which a worker performs her main job, has been collected only since the first quarter of 2018, which sets the start of the estimation window.

\paragraph{Linking individuals across quarters.} The public microdata carry no permanent personal identifier, so respondents must be linked across quarters from the information that is available: the household's position in the sample rotation and demographic characteristics that do not change over time, chiefly sex and date of birth. Exact matching on these fields recovers most individuals, but it fails whenever an interview records a birth date inconsistently or the household roster shifts between quarters, which is common enough to leave a meaningful share of respondents unmatched. I therefore use the advanced (Stage~3) identification procedure of the \href{https://github.com/datazoompuc/datazoom.social}{datazoom.social package} \citep{DataZoom2024} developed at PUC-Rio. Building on the panel-matching method of \citet{ribassoares2008}, originally devised for IBGE's Monthly Employment Survey, the procedure first imputes missing birth dates from other members of the same household and then, for records that still fail to match, applies a graph-based fuzzy match that tolerates small discrepancies in the reported day, month, and year of birth (the kind produced by typographical error) and selects the most consistent set of links. The raw quarterly microdata are obtained through the \href{https://cran.r-project.org/package=PNADcIBGE}{PNADcIBGE package}. Individuals the procedure cannot confidently match are assigned singleton identifiers and so contribute nothing to the within-person variation that identifies the fixed-effects estimates; the roughly 3.7\% of women in this position are excluded, which changes the number of observations but leaves the fixed-effects estimates numerically unchanged.

\paragraph{Panel quality.} Table~\ref{tab:panel_retention} documents that the reconstructed panel captures genuine repeated observations rather than spurious matches. It reports, for the estimation sample, the share of households and of matched individuals observed for at least a given number of quarters, together with the quarter-to-quarter continuation probability. The figures are computed on the analysis sample, prime-age women who head a household or are the head's spouse, and so reflect both survey attrition and the sample definition: a woman who ages out of the 18--49 window, or a household that no longer contains a qualifying woman, leaves the sample even though the survey continues to interview her. This is why retention is lower than an unrestricted population figure would be; it is a feature of the sample definition, not a failure of the matching. The key point for the identification is that a substantial share of women are observed repeatedly, so that individual fixed effects are meaningful.

\section{Variable definitions}\label{app:variables}

This section defines the derived variables used in the analysis that are not already described in the main text. All are constructed from the PNADC microdata, and each indicator equals one when the stated condition holds and zero otherwise.

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{In the labor force}: employed or actively searching for work; defined for every sample woman.
\item \textbf{Employed}: in the labor force and occupied. Women outside the labor force are coded zero rather than missing, so the outcome is the share employed among all women, not the share among labor-force participants.
\item \textbf{Formal / informal}: the formal indicator covers workers with a signed work card (private-sector, domestic, or public), the military and statutory civil servants, and self-employed workers who contribute to social security; all others are informal. It is broader than the CLT.
\item \textbf{CLT (\emph{celetista})}: signed-card employees in the private sector or in public companies and mixed-economy firms; this is the group Article 75-F directly binds. Statutory civil servants (governed by the separate public-service statute), the self-employed, and employers are excluded.
\item \textbf{Private / public employee}: an employee of a private-sector firm / of the public sector (including statutory civil servants), by the survey's position-in-employment classification.
\item \textbf{Telework-eligible occupation}: the worker's occupation is on the teleworkable list of \citet{goes2020} and \citet{costa2024}; see Appendix~\ref{app:telework}.
\item \textbf{Higher education}: has completed a tertiary degree.
\item \textbf{Youngest child's age in months}: used only for the exact-birthdate robustness in Table~\ref{tab:robustness}. The age of the household's youngest child, reconstructed from the reported month and year of birth and evaluated at the midpoint of the reference quarter; because the survey does not release the exact interview date, within-quarter timing leaves at most about six weeks of measurement error, and the day of birth is deliberately not used, as sub-monthly precision would be spurious. About 7\% of children under four lack a usable birth date, and their households drop from the exact-birthdate rows but remain in every completed-year specification, which is why the analysis uses completed-year age throughout and reserves the birth-date measure for that single check.
\end{itemize}

\section{Classifying telework-eligible occupations}\label{app:telework}

The mechanism analysis in Section~\ref{sec:mechanisms} relies on a classification of occupations into those that can, and those that cannot, plausibly be performed from home. I follow the now-standard approach of \citet{dingelneiman2020}, who use the physical and interpersonal task content of each occupation to determine whether its core activities require a physical presence at the workplace. Because that classification is built on United States occupation codes, it cannot be applied directly to the Brazilian data, which record each worker's occupation through the \emph{Classifica\c{c}\~ao de Ocupa\c{c}\~oes para Pesquisas Domiciliares} (COD), the four-digit household-survey occupation classification used by IBGE.\footnote{See \href{https://ftp.ibge.gov.br/Censos/Censo_Demografico_2010/metodologia/anexos/anexo_7_ocupacao_cod.pdf}{IBGE, \emph{Estrutura da Classifica\c{c}\~ao de Ocupa\c{c}\~oes para Pesquisas Domiciliares}}, which lists every COD code and its occupation title.}

I adopt the list of \citet{goes2020} and \citet{costa2024}, which classifies 126 four-digit COD occupations as teleworkable.\footnote{Transcribed occupation-by-occupation from the classification of \citet{goes2020} and \citet{costa2024}.} An occupation is treated as telework-eligible when it belongs to this set. Table~\ref{tab:telework_codes} lists the 126 codes grouped by one-digit COD major group, with representative occupations: they are overwhelmingly managerial, professional, technical, and administrative jobs, together with a small number of client-facing and printing-trade occupations. On the estimation sample the classification flags about 30\% of employed women (roughly 17\% of all sample women, whether employed or not) as holding a telework-eligible occupation, broadly in line with the roughly one-quarter of the Brazilian workforce that \citet{goes2020} classify as teleworkable. In the analysis, this classification enters only as a predetermined moderator (Table~\ref{tab:mech_moderation} splits the sample by each woman's eligibility at her \emph{last observation on or before the last pre-reform quarter}, held fixed, and restricts that table to women observed before the reform) and as the occupational-sorting outcome (Table~\ref{tab:mech_allocation}) of Section~\ref{sec:mechanisms}. The main specification always uses the full sample of eligible women; it never conditions on \emph{current} occupation, which, because occupation can respond to the reform, would be a post-treatment control.

\section{Validation of the home-based-work proxy}\label{app:validation}

The main outcome is an indicator for performing the main job at the worker's own residence. This measures the physical location of work rather than a contractual telework arrangement, so it captures employee telework together with home-based self-employment. To gauge how well it proxies for telework, I compare it with IBGE's experimental 2022 telework supplement, which asks employed persons whether, in 2022, they did remote work, telework, or telework performed at their residence, and reports the resulting shares by state. The supplement is released only as an aggregate state-level table, so the comparison is cross-sectional and cannot be carried out at the individual level; its universe (employed persons aged 14 or older, both sexes) also differs slightly from the estimation sample (aged 18--49), so the exercise concerns the spatial pattern and the order of magnitude rather than an exact level match.

Table~\ref{tab:proxy_validation} reports the comparison and Figure~\ref{fig:validation} plots it. Three facts emerge. First, the aggregate magnitudes agree: the proxy classifies about 6.5\% of the employed as working from home in 2022, close to the 7.2\% the supplement records as telework performed at home (Panel~A). Second, the proxy tracks the official measure across space: over the 27 states its employment-weighted correlation with the supplement's telework-at-home rate is $0.72$ (the unweighted correlation, $0.53$, is attenuated by the small northern states that the supplement itself flags as high-variance experimental estimates), and the states with the most telework (the Federal District, S\~ao Paulo, Rio de Janeiro) are also those where the proxy is highest (Figure~\ref{fig:validation}). Third, and most relevant to the interpretation, home-based work in the proxy is concentrated among the self-employed (about 19\% of them work from home, reflecting home businesses) and is low among the employees, and specifically the CLT employees, that Article~75-F binds (under 3\%; Panel~B). This is the measurement counterpart of the paper's central point: the location proxy overstates ``telework'' where the law does not reach and understates the reach of a priority that binds only CLT employees, which is why the effect is estimated separately on the predetermined CLT and teleworkable subgroups (Table~\ref{tab:estimands}).
