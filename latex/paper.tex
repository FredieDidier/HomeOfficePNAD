\begin{abstract}
\noindent Beginning in March 2022, Brazil required employers to prioritize employees with children aged four or younger when allocating remote-work positions. Using Brazil's quarterly household panel for 2018--2026, I compare mothers of children aged 0--4 with mothers of children aged 5--7 in a difference-in-differences design. The population-average estimate for home-based work is $-0.08$ percentage points, with a 95\% confidence interval of $[-0.67, 0.51]$ on a pre-reform mean of 4.6\%. Estimates for the employees the statute covers and for workers in teleworkable occupations are statistically indistinguishable from zero but substantially less precise. I find no occupational sorting toward teleworkable jobs and no differential response by gender. The results indicate no detectable population-wide increase in home-based work, while leaving economically relevant effects among legally covered workers unresolved. Soft, weakly enforced flexibility mandates may have limited aggregate reach where formal teleworkable jobs are scarce.

\vspace{0.5em}
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} telework; work from home; gender; family policy; child penalty; Brazil

\vspace{0.3em}
\noindent\textbf{JEL codes:} J16; J22; K31
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% =============================================================================

The arrival and persistence of a young child remain one of the sharpest turning points in a woman's working life. Across high- and middle-income countries, mothers' earnings and employment fall abruptly at the birth of a first child and recover slowly, if at all, while fathers' trajectories are largely undisturbed \citep{kleven2019denmark,kleven2019countries}. In Brazil the tension between paid work and young children is severe: women's labor-force participation stands roughly 20 percentage points below men's, women perform close to twice as many weekly hours of unpaid domestic work and caregiving, and the employment rate of mothers with a child under 3 is well below that of women without young children \citep{ibge_gender}. At the same time, the pandemic made working from home a mainstream possibility for a meaningful minority of jobs and put workplace flexibility at the center of the policy debate on gender gaps \citep{barrero2023,dingelneiman2020}. Whether flexibility can be legislated into the hands of the workers who need it most is, therefore, a first-order question for population and family economics.

Workplace flexibility is a valuable job amenity, but access to it depends jointly on worker preferences, job technology, and employer allocation. This paper asks whether a legal priority can expand access to that amenity when the mandate neither creates teleworkable positions nor removes employer discretion, and when much of the target population works outside formal employment. On the demand side, \citet{goldin2014} argues that a large part of the remaining gender gap reflects the disproportionate reward that firms attach to long, inflexible hours, so that arrangements which relax the time constraint, telework prominent among them, are precisely what could narrow it. Workers, in turn, value such arrangements, and by the logic of compensating differentials \citep{rosen1986} will trade wages for them: \citet{maspallais2017} show that the average worker is willing to give up a non-trivial fraction of wages to avoid rigid schedules, though this average masks wide heterogeneity (many workers would not pay for flexibility while a minority value it highly), and inferring such valuations from observed wage differentials is itself difficult \citep{lavetti2023}. A growing body of causal evidence finds that home and hybrid work can raise, rather than lower, productivity and retention \citep{bloom2015,bloomhanliang2024}, even as access to remote work is shaped by selection as well as treatment \citep{emanuelharrington2024}. Yet the instrument at issue here is not a subsidy or an entitlement but a mandated priority, and the economics of mandated benefits warns that such mandates bind only weakly when compliance is costly and hard to verify \citep{summers1989,gruber1994}. When the law merely instructs employers to ``prioritize'' a group for a benefit that many jobs cannot supply, and provides no enforcement, standard incidence logic predicts little movement in equilibrium allocations.

This paper provides the first evidence on whether Brazil's telework-priority mandate changed the labor-market outcomes of the women it was meant to protect. In March 2022, through Provisional Measure 1108 and later Law 14.442, Brazil amended the Consolidation of Labor Laws (Article 75-F) to require employers to give priority in the allocation of telework-eligible positions to employees with children or legal dependents up to 4 years of age.\footnote{Provisional Measures have the force of law on publication; Provisional Measure 1108 took effect on 28 March 2022 and was converted, without change to Article 75-F, into Law 14.442 on 2 September 2022. See \href{https://www.planalto.gov.br/ccivil_03/_ato2019-2022/2022/mpv/mpv1108.htm}{Provisional Measure 1108/2022} and \href{https://www.planalto.gov.br/ccivil_03/_ato2019-2022/2022/lei/l14442.htm}{Law 14.442/2022}.} I evaluate the reform using the rotating panel of the Continuous National Household Sample Survey (PNAD Cont\'inua), which follows individuals for up to five consecutive quarters and, since 2018, records whether each worker performs her main job at home.

The design is a difference-in-differences framework around the reform, with individual fixed effects that absorb time-invariant selection into motherhood. It compares women whose youngest child is aged 4 or younger, who are eligible for the priority, with women whose youngest child is aged 5--7, who are similar in their selection into motherhood but just above the statutory threshold. The main outcome is whether a woman performs her main job at her own residence, which I call home-based work; because it is measured over all women, with the non-employed coded zero, the coefficient is an effect on the population share working from home. Since I observe legal eligibility rather than whether any employer granted remote work, the estimate is an intent-to-treat effect of \emph{having} a young child. And because the sample includes many women the statute cannot reach (the informal, the self-employed, statutory civil servants, and the non-employed), the headline coefficient is a population-average effect of exposure to the reform, not the effect among the CLT (\emph{celetista}) employees Article 75-F legally binds. Those covered employees are registered private-sector workers together with the employees of public companies and mixed-economy firms, who are hired under the CLT and so covered on the same basis. I report both: the population-average effect, and, separately, the effect estimated among women who were CLT employees, or in teleworkable occupations, before the reform.

I find no effect. The share of age-eligible women working from home did not rise relative to the comparison group after the reform: the difference-in-differences estimate is $-0.08$ percentage points on a pre-reform base of about 5\%, and at the 95\% level I can rule out increases larger than about 0.5 percentage points. An event study shows flat pre-reform trends, including through the 2020--2021 pandemic, and, at most, a small effect emerging only two to three years later. A placebo supports the design: mothers whose youngest child is 5--7, whom the law does not cover, show no change relative to women with no young children at all, so it is the age-4 threshold, and not a broader difference between mothers and other women, that identifies the effect.

Additional results probe whether the null masks offsetting responses. Point estimates are not positive among women who were CLT employees or in telework-eligible occupations before the reform, but the substantially wider confidence intervals for these smaller, legally covered groups do not rule out economically meaningful effects there; and treated mothers did not move into telework-eligible occupations after the reform, so there is no ``priority-seeking'' occupational sorting. The effect is likewise indistinguishable from zero across subgroups: for formal and informal workers, for the CLT employees the statute binds (private and public) and those outside the CLT, and across education, race, household structure (including single mothers), and age. The null survives alternative treatment and control definitions, dropping the pandemic years, state-by-year-quarter fixed effects, and alternative clustering. The same null appears when eligible and comparison mothers are matched narrowly on the child's age around the threshold, and even for the youngest children, for whom the demand for telework is greatest. It is also robust to a triple-differences design that adds men with young children as a third comparison group: because the statute is gender-neutral, these men are themselves eligible, so the design does not treat them as an untreated control but tests whether the young-child differential moved more for women than for men. It did not: I find no detectable female-specific response relative to eligible men.

The pattern points to an institutional explanation. A priority is not a right: it is only as valuable as the stock of teleworkable positions an employer has to allocate, and for most age-eligible women, who work informally, in the public sector, or in jobs that cannot be done from home, that stock is close to empty. That population narrows further at exactly the life stage the reform targets, because motherhood itself pushes women toward informal work. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} develops this reading and weighs it against alternatives.

The paper contributes to three literatures. The first is the economics of gender and workplace flexibility, in which telework, as an arrangement that relaxes the time constraint behind much of the gender gap, is a leading candidate to narrow it \citep{goldin2014,maspallais2017,bloom2015,flabbimoro2012} and whose prevalence the pandemic durably raised \citep{barrero2023,dingelneiman2020}. Against this backdrop, a recurring hope is that widening access to flexible arrangements can compress gender gaps \citep{bertrand2011,blaukahn2017}; the present evidence shows that access cannot be legislated into existence when the underlying jobs are not teleworkable and compliance is voluntary.

The second is the literature on the effectiveness of family policy and its relation to the child penalty \citep{kleven2019denmark,kleven2019countries,klevenatlas2025,addadustmann2017,olivettipetrongolo2017,angelov2016,lundborg2017}, which has largely evaluated leave, cash transfers, and childcare. Telework priority is a newer and far cheaper instrument that governments increasingly reach for,\footnote{Statutory rights to flexible or remote work for parents and carers have spread internationally. In the European Union, the \href{https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/dir/2019/1158/oj}{Work-Life Balance Directive (EU) 2019/1158} requires member states to grant workers with young children and caring responsibilities the right to request flexible working arrangements, including remote work; in the United States, jurisdictions such as San Francisco (the \href{https://www.sf.gov/information--family-friendly-workplace-ordinance}{Family Friendly Workplace Ordinance}) have adopted comparable right-to-request rules.} and evidence on whether it works is scarce; documenting that a well-identified version of it did not even raise home-based work, the outcome it most directly targets, is directly useful for the design of family and flexible-work policy, on which there is still limited causal evidence in middle-income labor markets.

The third is the economics of group-specific mandated benefits and their enforcement \citep{summers1989,gruber1994}, which has shown that a mandate targeted at an identifiable group can shift that group's wages or employment. Closest to the present study, \citet{battagliabrown2025} evaluate pregnancy-accommodation laws, another group-specific accommodation mandate aimed at women, with an analogous research design, a difference-in-differences reinforced by a triple difference that adds men as a comparison group, and find no effect on women's employment or wages. The present case reinforces and sharpens theirs: because I observe the take-up margin directly, I can show that the priority does not even raise home-based work, so a benefit this weakly binding, and this poorly matched to the jobs the target group actually holds, moves nothing. It does so in a middle-income labor market with a large informal sector and limited enforcement \citep{ulyssea2018,gerardgonzaga2021,almeidacarneiro2012}, precisely the setting where soft mandates are most likely to fall flat. The paper therefore speaks to the implementability of non-wage job amenities in segmented labor markets: whether a low-enforcement mandate can reallocate a scarce amenity that workers may value but firms need not create.

The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section~\ref{sec:background} describes the reform, its institutional setting, and the theoretical impact of the priority, Section~\ref{sec:data} the data and sample, and Section~\ref{sec:strategy} the empirical strategy. Section~\ref{sec:results} presents the results: the effect on home-based work, the reduced-form outcomes, the mechanisms, the heterogeneity analysis, and the robustness, inference, and triple-difference checks. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses the findings and Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

\section{Institutional background}\label{sec:background}
% =============================================================================

\subsection{The reform}

Brazilian labor law had no dedicated treatment of telework until 2017, when a broad labor reform added a chapter on remote work to the Consolidation of Labor Laws (CLT).\footnote{See \href{https://www.planalto.gov.br/ccivil_03/_ato2015-2018/2017/lei/l13467.htm}{Law 13.467/2017}, the 2017 labor reform, which added Chapter II-A (``Do Teletrabalho'') to the CLT.} The arrangement remained marginal until the pandemic forced a temporary but massive shift to home-based work in 2020--2021.\footnote{Home-based work covered about 5.8\% of the employed in 2019 (IBGE's PNAD Cont\'inua, reported by \href{https://www.cnnbrasil.com.br/economia/macroeconomia/trabalho-remoto-mais-que-dobrou-em-10-anos-mas-caiu-apos-pandemia-mostra-ibge/}{CNN Brasil}); it then jumped with the pandemic, reaching about 8.7 million workers at the May 2020 peak and averaging roughly 11\% of the employed over 2020 (IBGE's PNAD COVID-19 survey; see \href{https://www.ipea.gov.br/cartadeconjuntura/index.php/2021/02/o-trabalho-remoto-e-a-pandemia-o-que-a-pnad-covid-19-nos-mostrou/}{IPEA, \emph{Carta de Conjuntura}, 2021}).} As that shock receded, the federal government issued Provisional Measure 1108 on 25 March 2022 (published 28 March), which modernized the telework chapter and, in its Article 75-F, introduced the provision studied here;\footnote{The statute treats \emph{teletrabalho} and \emph{trabalho remoto} (telework and remote work) as synonyms (CLT, Art. 75-B, as amended), and I likewise use \emph{telework}, \emph{remote work}, and \emph{working from home} interchangeably throughout. The outcome is measured as performing one's main job at the worker's own residence (Section~\ref{sec:data}).} the measure was converted, with the article unchanged, into Law 14.442 on 2 September 2022.

Article 75-F directs employers to give priority, in allocating positions whose activities can be performed remotely, to employees with disabilities and to employees with children or legal dependents up to 4 years of age. Three features of the provision shape the analysis. First, it grants a priority in allocation, not a right to telework: an employer must place eligible employees ahead of others when distributing positions that are already teleworkable, but need not create such positions or grant remote work to anyone in particular. Second, it carries no dedicated enforcement mechanism or penalty and requires neither an employee request nor any certification. Third, it binds only on employees under the CLT (registered employees in the private sector and in public companies and mixed-economy firms, which hire under the CLT), and not on statutory civil servants, the self-employed, employers, or the large population of workers without a registered contract. Together these features make Article 75-F a near-textbook soft mandate,\footnote{Published labor-court decisions indicate that Article~75-F has occasionally been invoked in disputes over remote-work requests, including by mothers of children with serious health conditions, and, notably, by public-company \emph{celetista} employees such as a postal-service worker and a public bank clerk (\href{https://mpmt.mp.br/portalcao/news/1013/130543/mae-com-filho-pequeno-ou-com-deficiencia-tem-prioridade-em-teletrabalho/916}{MPMT, 2023}; \href{https://www.trt18.jus.br/portal/escrituraria-obtem-teletrabalho-para-acompanhamento-de-filha-com-grave-alergia-alimentar/}{TRT-18, 2026}).} and they motivate both the intent-to-treat reading of the estimates (Section~\ref{sec:strategy}) and the placebo splits by contract type (Section~\ref{sec:heterogeneity}). At the same time, the entitlement is not a dead letter: eligible workers have gone to the labor courts to invoke Article~75-F and obtain or keep remote work. That some value the benefit enough to litigate for it, together with the strong latent demand for workplace flexibility among mothers of young children documented below, means the null cannot be read as a mere absence of demand for the benefit. The reach of the priority is nonetheless narrow (Section~\ref{sec:discussion}), so the contribution is not that a large aggregate effect was expected, but that, despite this demonstrated demand, the reform produced no detectable population-wide increase in home-based work and no positive sign of an effect even in the groups the law can reach.

\subsection{Theoretical impact of the priority on labor-market outcomes}\label{sec:framework}

A telework priority is a group-specific mandated benefit, and its potential effects can be organized around a single implementation outcome. If the priority binds, eligible mothers move to the front of the queue for teleworkable positions, so its immediate, mechanical effect is to raise their probability of working from home. This is the outcome the statute acts on most directly, and the other outcomes it could touch (earnings, hours, employment continuity, fertility) are plausibly downstream of it: telework may let a mother stay employed through her child's early years, change her hours or her exposure to the motherhood earnings penalty, or shift the timing of births. The direction of these downstream effects is theoretically ambiguous \citep{goldin2014,olivettipetrongolo2017}. A null effect on measured home-based work therefore weakens the hypothesized telework mechanism, but does not by itself logically rule out effects operating through unmeasured remote-work arrangements, bargaining, retention, employer behavior, or offsetting heterogeneous responses; each of those is examined separately below. The analysis accordingly centers on home-based work as the outcome the priority most directly governs, while reading the reduced-form outcomes on their own terms rather than as bounded by it.

Whether the priority binds at all is in doubt, for two reasons the mandated-benefits literature makes familiar. First, Article 75-F is an unusually soft mandate: it confers a priority rather than a right, attaches no penalty, and requires neither a request nor a certification, so an employer can comply by doing nothing and an eligible worker can hold the entitlement without ever invoking it. Mandates that protect an identifiable group can fail for exactly this reason: employment protection under the Americans with Disabilities Act, a similarly group-specific mandate, did not raise disabled workers' employment and by some estimates lowered it, because compliance was costly to enforce and easy to avoid \citep{acemogluangrist2001}, and this one sits near the same limiting case. Second, and specific to telework, a priority in allocating teleworkable positions is worth only as much as the stock of such positions an employer has to allocate; for the many age-eligible women who work informally, in the public sector, or in occupations that cannot be performed from home, that stock is close to empty, so the priority has nothing to act on.

These features yield the paper's testable structure. If the priority binds where it can, the effect on home-based work should be positive and concentrated among women in teleworkable occupations and under CLT contracts (the workers the statute reaches), and downstream outcomes should move in step. If instead the mandate is too soft or the teleworkable jobs too scarce, the effect on home-based work is zero everywhere, and there is little for downstream outcomes to run through. The results below adjudicate between these cases.

\section{Data}\label{sec:data}
% =============================================================================

The analysis draws on the Continuous National Household Sample Survey (PNADC), the rotating quarterly household survey run by the Brazilian Institute of Geography and Statistics (IBGE) and the source of Brazil's official labor-market statistics. Each selected household is interviewed in up to five consecutive quarters, which yields a short individual-level panel. I use every quarter from the first quarter of 2018 (the first in which the survey records where each worker performs her main job, the variable behind the home-based-work outcome) through the first quarter of 2026. Individuals are linked across quarters from the survey's household and person identifiers together with an advanced matching procedure that reconstructs the panel even when interviews are incomplete; Appendix~\ref{app:pnadc} details the survey and the panel construction, and Appendix Table~\ref{tab:panel_retention} shows that the reconstructed panel captures genuine repeated observations of the same individuals and households.

The estimation sample is women aged 18--49 who are the household head or the head's spouse or partner, and whom the matching procedure links across quarters. The age range is the standard prime-age window for female labor supply: 18 marks entry into the adult labor market, and capping at 49 keeps the sample to working-age women and keeps the treated and comparison groups demographically comparable, rather than mixing in near-retirement women whose employment responds to different margins. Restricting to heads and spouses ensures that a young child in the household reflects the woman's own caregiving responsibility (for a child, stepchild, or grandchild in her care) rather than another household member's dependent.

The main outcome is an indicator for home-based work, defined as performing the main job at the worker's own residence. Two features of this measure should be stated plainly. First, it records the physical \emph{location} of the main job, not the contractual arrangement behind it: the survey asks whether the job is performed at the worker's residence (with or without a dedicated workspace), which captures formal telework but also home-based businesses and own-account work, so I read it as home-based work as measured in the PNADC rather than as telework in the contractual sense. Appendix~\ref{app:validation} validates the proxy against IBGE's experimental 2022 telework supplement: the two measures agree in aggregate (about 6--7\% of the employed) and are positively correlated across the 27 states (employment-weighted correlation $0.72$). Second, non-employed women are coded zero rather than missing, so the outcome is the share of \emph{all} women working from home; I therefore also report it restricted to the employed and to narrower contract groups (Section~\ref{sec:results}).

The remaining outcomes are real monthly labor earnings (in constant reais of the first quarter of 2026, the most recent quarter, deflated with IBGE's official PNADC deflator), usual weekly hours, indicators for employment and for labor-force participation, and an indicator for being on maternity leave, a proxy for a recent birth.\footnote{Earnings and usual hours observed among workers are conditional outcomes and may be compositionally affected if employment responds to the reform. I therefore report both the worker-conditional specifications and unconditional earnings and hours defined over all women, coding the non-employed zero (Table~\ref{tab:earnings_margins}).} Fertility itself is not observed in the survey, so I do not estimate effects on childbearing; maternity leave enters only as a coarse proxy for a recent birth, and it is not interpreted causally (Section~\ref{sec:results}). The other derived variables (labor-force and employment status, contract type, education, and telework eligibility) are defined in Appendix~\ref{app:variables}. All statistics and estimates use the survey's sampling weights.

Treatment is defined by the presence in the household of a child aged 4 or younger, for a woman who is head or spouse. The primary comparison group is women whose youngest child is aged 5--7: mothers who are similar on the margins that select women into motherhood but whose youngest child has just crossed the statutory age-4 cutoff, so that comparing them with treated women isolates the effect of the threshold rather than of motherhood itself. A second comparison group, women with no child aged 0--7, serves two further purposes: it is larger, which sharpens precision, and it anchors a placebo. If mothers of children aged 5--7, whom the law does not cover, behave no differently from women with no young children at all, then the 5--7 group is a clean control and the design is not merely picking up a generic difference between mothers and other women; this is the placebo I report in Appendix Table~\ref{tab:robustness}.

An occupation is classified as telework-eligible following the adaptation of \citet{dingelneiman2020} to Brazilian occupation codes by \citet{goes2020} and \citet{costa2024}, described in Appendix~\ref{app:telework}. A natural instinct would be to restrict the sample to the workers the priority can most plausibly reach: those in telework-eligible occupations, or the CLT employees that Article 75-F directly binds. Both margins, however, can respond to the reform itself (an eligible woman may move into a teleworkable occupation to claim the priority, or into or out of formal, registered employment), so conditioning the \emph{contemporaneous} sample on either would mean controlling for a post-treatment outcome, a bad control \citep{angristpischke2009}. I therefore keep the full sample of age-eligible women as the headline population and use occupation and contract type as \emph{predetermined} classifications instead: I report the effect separately on the subsample of women who were CLT employees, or in teleworkable occupations, at their last observation on or before the last pre-reform quarter, which isolates the effect among the workers Article 75-F can legally reach without conditioning on any post-reform outcome (Section~\ref{sec:firststage}). Occupation additionally enters as an outcome.

The statute covers children ``up to 4 years of age'', which means a child who has not yet reached the fifth birthday, so treatment is the presence of a child of completed age 0 to 4 and the comparison group begins at completed age 5. The survey records age in completed years, which places the youngest child in a one-year bracket but does not pin down its exact position relative to the four-year boundary: a completed-age-4 child is between four and five years old, and under a stricter reading of the statute (eligibility ending on the fourth rather than the fifth birthday) some of these children would belong in the comparison group. To show that the null does not depend on where this line is drawn, I reconstruct each child's age in months from the reported month and year of birth (defined in Appendix~\ref{app:variables}), anchored at the midpoint of the reference quarter since the survey does not release the exact interview date, and re-estimate the effect on home-based work while sliding the eligibility ceiling across the boundary, from a strict ``until the fourth birthday'' cutoff (48 months) up to the baseline ``until the fifth birthday'' definition (60 months). The estimate is small and statistically insignificant throughout (Section~\ref{sec:robustness}).

Table~\ref{tab:descriptives} reports summary statistics by group and period, and two patterns stand out. Working from home is uncommon in every group (about 5\% before the reform) and rises only modestly afterward, in parallel across groups; Figure~\ref{fig:trends} shows this quarter by quarter. Treated women are younger, participate less in the labor market, and are less likely to hold a formal job than the comparison groups. Identification does not require the groups to match on these levels: the individual fixed effects difference out time-invariant differences between them, and the design rests on parallel trends rather than on identical composition (Section~\ref{sec:strategy}).

\section{Empirical strategy}\label{sec:strategy}
% =============================================================================

I estimate the effect of eligibility with a two-way fixed-effects difference-in-differences model. For woman $i$ in year-quarter $t$,
%
\begin{equation}
y_{it} = \beta\,(\text{YoungChild}_{it}\times \text{Post}_t) + \gamma\,\text{YoungChild}_{it} + \alpha_i + \delta_t + \varepsilon_{it},
\label{eq:did}
\end{equation}
%
where $\text{YoungChild}_{it}$ equals one when woman $i$ has a child aged 4 or younger in quarter $t$, $\text{Post}_t$ equals one from the second quarter of 2022 onward, $\alpha_i$ are individual fixed effects, and $\delta_t$ are year-quarter fixed effects. The coefficient of interest is $\beta$. For the binary outcomes (home-based work and the employment, labor-force-participation, and maternity-leave indicators), Equation~\eqref{eq:did} is a linear probability model estimated by ordinary least squares, so $\beta$ is an effect on the probability of the outcome, reported throughout in percentage points. Because eligibility is time-varying, in that a woman is treated only in the quarters in which she has a young child, the contemporaneous term $\text{YoungChild}_{it}$ must be included: individual fixed effects do not absorb it, and omitting it would load the level ``motherhood penalty'' of women who transition into young-child status onto $\beta$. Standard errors are clustered at the household level and all regressions are weighted by the survey weights.\footnote{Because the reform date is common to all units rather than staggered across them, Equation~\eqref{eq:did} is a generalized two-way fixed-effects difference-in-differences that involves no comparisons between cohorts treated at different times, and so is not exposed to the negative-weighting problem that such comparisons can create in staggered designs \citep{goodmanbacon2021,dechaisemartin2020}. The only time variation in treatment is in eligibility itself, which switches on and off as a child crosses the age-4 threshold and is absorbed by the contemporaneous $\text{YoungChild}_{it}$ term. A single common post-period likewise makes the standard event study an adequate diagnostic without a heterogeneity-robust estimator.}

The identifying assumption is that, absent the reform, home-based-work rates for mothers of children aged 4 or younger and for the comparison group would have evolved in parallel. Individual fixed effects make this assumption considerably more credible than a cross-sectional comparison, because they remove all time-invariant differences between women who have a child aged 4 or younger and women whose youngest child is 5--7. The remaining threat, differential trends, is examined directly with an event study that traces the treated-versus-control gap quarter by quarter, normalized to the last pre-reform quarter.

The estimate recovers an intent-to-treat effect. The survey does not reveal whether a given employer reassigned a given woman to remote work because of the law; it reveals eligibility and realized outcomes. Comparing eligible and non-eligible women around the reform therefore identifies the effect of \emph{having} a young child on home-based work, regardless of take-up. Two things about the estimand follow. First, because the sample includes women the statute cannot legally reach (the informal, the self-employed, statutory civil servants, and the non-employed), the coefficient $\beta$ in Equation~\eqref{eq:did} is a population-average effect of exposure to the reform, not the effect among the covered employees; it is therefore diluted by women who could never have received the priority. This is a feature of the policy question (how much the reform moved the population it nominally targeted), but to recover the effect where the law can bind I re-estimate Equation~\eqref{eq:did} on predetermined subsamples of CLT and teleworkable women in Section~\ref{sec:firststage}. Second, home-based work is the outcome the priority most directly governs, but a null on it does not mechanically bound the reduced-form outcomes, which I read on their own terms.

\section{Results}\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Home-based work: the primary implementation outcome}\label{sec:firststage}

Table~\ref{tab:did_firststage} reports the effect on home-based work through a specification ladder. Column~1 is a raw regression with neither controls nor fixed effects; column~2 adds demographic controls; columns~3 and~4 add year-quarter fixed effects and then individual fixed effects; column~5 adds a quadratic in age. Across the first three columns the estimate is a marginally significant $+0.4$ percentage points, and neither observed demographics nor year-quarter fixed effects move it. Adding individual fixed effects (column~4) collapses it to $-0.08$ points and removes its significance, and adding age controls leaves it unchanged. The cross-sectional association reflects selection into young motherhood on time-invariant characteristics that observed demographics do not capture but individual fixed effects absorb, rather than an effect of the law; once that selection is netted out, no effect remains. The same contrast appears with a different estimator: a repeated-cross-section difference-in-differences that drops the individual fixed effects and instead absorbs the youngest child's age, the mother's age, and state-by-year-quarter fixed effects returns the same $+0.35$-point cross-sectional gap ($p<0.01$; Appendix Table~\ref{tab:inference}), confirming that what separates the naive positive from the null is the person-level selection the individual fixed effects remove, not the choice of estimator.

\input{../analysis/output/tables/tab02_did_firststage.tex}

The preferred estimate (column~4) is $-0.08$ percentage points, with a 95\% confidence interval of roughly $[-0.67,\,0.51]$ on a pre-reform base of about 5\%: the population-average effect rules out increases above about half a percentage point. I do not overstate this as exact equivalence to zero. A two-one-sided-tests equivalence test against a pre-specified margin of $0.5$ percentage points does not quite reject an effect that large ($p=0.08$), so the accurate statement is that the data exclude increases above roughly half a point rather than that they establish equivalence at that margin.

\paragraph{Population-average versus legally-covered estimands.} The coefficient above is a population-average effect, estimated over all age-eligible women, most of whom (informal, self-employed, statutory civil servants, or not employed) the statute cannot reach, so it is mechanically diluted. Table~\ref{tab:reach} makes the dilution concrete: among women with a child aged 0--4, only about one in five is a CLT employee, and only about 6\% are CLT employees in a telework-eligible occupation who are not already working from home, the group the priority could plausibly move. Table~\ref{tab:estimands} re-estimates the effect on subsamples defined by each woman's status \emph{before} the reform (at her last observation on or before the last pre-reform quarter, so the classification is predetermined and not itself an outcome). The effect is small and statistically insignificant for all women, and it remains statistically indistinguishable from zero for the employed, for salaried employees, for the CLT employees the law directly binds, private and public (about $-1.3$ points), for the self-employed, for women in teleworkable occupations, and for the intersection of CLT and teleworkable employment. Two cautions temper this. First, the point estimates in the covered subgroups are, if anything, mildly negative, the opposite of what a binding priority would produce. Second, these subsamples are far smaller, so their confidence intervals are correspondingly wider: the CLT estimate is consistent with anything in $[-3.5,\,0.9]$ percentage points and the CLT-by-teleworkable estimate with a still wider range, and the equivalence test is inconclusive at the one-point margin (Table~\ref{tab:equivalence}). The population-average estimate is informative about large increases, although it does not establish equivalence at the pre-specified $0.5$-point margin. The point estimates among legally covered workers are not positive, but the confidence intervals are too wide to establish either equivalence or the absence of economically meaningful effects.

\paragraph{Event study.} Figure~\ref{fig:event_home} plots the estimated treated-versus-control gap in home-based work by quarter, for both comparison groups, normalized to the first quarter of 2022. The pre-reform coefficients are flat and close to zero throughout, including during the 2020--2021 pandemic, and a joint test does not reject that they are all zero (see the figure note), which supports the parallel-trends assumption. After the reform the gap remains near zero for roughly two years and drifts up to about one point in a few quarters of 2024--2025. This late movement does not survive aggregation: splitting the post-period into an early (2022--2023) and a late (2024--2026) window, in a single regression, gives effects of $-0.2$ and $+0.2$ percentage points, neither significant, and even the late window rules out increases larger than about 0.9 percentage points (Appendix Table~\ref{tab:robustness}). Whatever its source (the reform became permanent law only in September 2022, and telework arrangements are renegotiated slowly), the late drift leaves the pooled null intact.

\subsection{Secondary labor-market outcomes}

The reform's remaining labor-market outcomes are secondary to home-based work and are reported in Appendix Table~\ref{tab:did_outcomes_A}. Which of them can be read causally is settled by the same parallel-trends diagnostic that validates the home-based-work null, not asserted: a joint test of the pre-reform event-study leads rejects parallel trends for employment ($\chi^2(16)=39.6$), labor-force participation ($\chi^2(16)=38.0$), and maternity leave ($\chi^2(16)=282$), all $p<0.01$, but not for home-based work ($\chi^2(16)=12.6$, $p=0.70$). The small significant coefficients on employment and participation (about $+1$ point each) and on maternity leave (about $-0.9$ points) therefore track differential trends rather than an effect of the reform and are not interpreted causally; the maternity-leave event study (Appendix Figure~\ref{fig:event_maternity}) shows the pattern directly, with the treated-versus-control gap already declining well before 2022.

Earnings and usual hours are observed only for workers, so those regressions condition on employment, a potentially post-treatment variable; Appendix Table~\ref{tab:earnings_margins} reports each twice, among workers and re-defined over all women with the non-employed coded zero. Among employed women the estimates for earnings and hours are small and statistically insignificant and exhibit flat pre-reform conditional trends ($\chi^2(16)=8.7$, $p=0.93$ for log earnings; $\chi^2(16)=9.4$, $p=0.90$ for hours). Because employment is potentially post-treatment, however, these worker-conditional estimates remain subject to compositional selection and are interpreted as secondary conditional results rather than clean causal effects; their unconditional counterparts, which fold in the employment extensive margin, are also small but fail the diagnostic ($\chi^2(16)=33.3$ and $36.3$, both $p<0.01$). In short, the secondary analyses find small conditional estimates for earnings and hours, while employment, participation, maternity leave, and the unconditional earnings and hours measures fail the parallel-trends diagnostic and are not interpreted causally. Finally, the placebo that compares the two control groups (mothers of children 5--7 versus women with no young children, neither of whom the law touches) is $+0.04$ points and insignificant (Appendix Table~\ref{tab:robustness}), so it is the age threshold, rather than some generic difference between mothers and non-mothers, that the design exploits.

\subsection{Implementation margins: predetermined teleworkability and occupational sorting}\label{sec:mechanisms}

These exercises do not separately identify why the mandate failed to change home-based work. They test two observable margins on which implementation could appear: whether effects are concentrated among workers whose pre-reform jobs were technically compatible with remote work, especially those also covered by the CLT, and whether young-child women subsequently sort into teleworkable occupations. Neither margin is present in the data.

Table~\ref{tab:mech_moderation} splits the home-based-work effect by whether a woman was in a telework-eligible occupation before the reform, measured at her last observation on or before the last pre-reform quarter and held fixed so that it is not itself an outcome. The effect is no different from zero among the eligible (a group for whom technical feasibility is necessary but not sufficient, since legal coverage additionally requires an eligible CLT employment relationship) and among the non-eligible. Table~\ref{tab:mech_allocation} takes the natural next step and uses telework eligibility of the current occupation as the outcome. A reform that pushed mothers to seek telework-friendly jobs in order to claim the priority would produce a positive coefficient; the estimate is essentially zero for both comparison groups. Appendix Table~\ref{tab:occ_transition} makes the same point descriptively: among women observed both before and after the reform, the pattern of occupation switches into and out of telework eligibility is nearly identical for treated and control mothers. There is, in short, no behavioral response of occupational choice to the priority.

\subsection{Heterogeneity}\label{sec:heterogeneity}

Table~\ref{tab:heterogeneity} reports the home-based-work effect across subgroups, all defined at a predetermined baseline, and Appendix Figure~\ref{fig:hetero} summarizes the same estimates visually. The splits by contract type are the most informative, because the statute binds only on CLT employees: if the estimate reflected Article 75-F rather than a generic shock to mothers of young children, it should appear for the CLT employees it binds and vanish for the informal, whom it cannot reach. A further split isolates single mothers, household heads with no co-resident partner. They are arguably the group with the strongest latent demand for the benefit: unable to share childcare with a partner, they place a premium on job flexibility and proximity, of which telework is the limiting case \citep{berniell2023,maspallais2017,lebarbanchon2021}. They are also the group for whom the employment friction created by mandated maternity protections is most salient \citep{gruber1994}. If the priority bound anywhere, it should bind for them.

Instead the estimate is indistinguishable from zero across almost the entire table: for formal and informal workers, for the CLT employees the statute binds and for those outside the CLT, and across education, race, age, and household structure. Of the fifteen subgroups, exactly one is individually significant: single mothers, at about $-2.6$ percentage points. That estimate is, if anything, wrong-signed. Were the priority raising home-based work among the women who most want it, its effect would be positive and largest precisely for single mothers, so a negative coefficient is the opposite of what a demand-driven take-up of the benefit would produce. It is also not located where the statute could act. Decomposing the single-mother cell by contract type, at Table~\ref{tab:heterogeneity}, shows it is concentrated among single mothers outside the CLT, at about $-3.2$ percentage points, whom the law cannot reach, and is an insignificant $-0.3$ percentage points among the CLT single mothers it does bind. Because this decomposition splits one already-listed cell rather than adding new subgroup hypotheses (the single-mother cell is mechanically the union of its two parts), it is reported as an interpretive channel check and held out of the multiple-testing family. A movement confined to the group the law cannot touch cannot be its effect.\footnote{The single-mother subgroup also shows flat pre-reform trends: a joint Wald test of the pre-reform event-study leads does not reject parallel trends ($\chi^2(16)=19.3$, $p=0.25$), so the negative coefficient is not a differential-trend artifact either; it is confined to the group the statute cannot bind and, as reported next, does not survive the correction for multiple testing.}

This is exactly what testing many subgroups invites: under the null, the more cuts one takes, the more likely that some estimate crosses a conventional threshold by chance, so a single ``significant'' subgroup, wrong-signed and confined to workers the law cannot reach, says little unless it survives a correction for the number of tests. It does not. Table~\ref{tab:heterogeneity} reports, for each subgroup, the raw $p$-value alongside a Holm step-down adjustment for multiple testing \citep{holm1979}: once the fifteen tests are accounted for, no subgroup is significant (the smallest adjusted $p$-value is $0.44$), and the one flagged cell is neither predicted by theory nor located where the law binds. The heterogeneity analysis thus does not rescue a hidden effect: no subgroup point estimate provides evidence of an increase. This is not the same as establishing equivalence everywhere: the estimates for the CLT and teleworkable employees the statute directly binds are the least precise, so a modest effect concentrated there cannot be ruled out (Table~\ref{tab:estimands}). Still, the data give no positive sign of one, including among single mothers and in the groups where the law was supposed to bite hardest.

\subsection{Robustness}\label{sec:robustness}

Appendix Table~\ref{tab:robustness} collects robustness checks on the home-based-work effect. The null is unchanged when the post-period starts one quarter earlier, when the treatment definition excludes grandchildren, stepchildren, or both, when the pandemic years are dropped so that a clean 2018--2019 pre-period is compared with the post-reform quarters, and when state-by-year-quarter fixed effects absorb region-specific shocks. It is also unchanged under two-way clustering, which allows for correlation not only within a household but also across neighboring households in the same primary sampling unit, the census enumeration area that the survey draws within each geographic stratum.

Restricting the sample to women in a telework-eligible occupation at baseline (defined as in Table~\ref{tab:mech_moderation}, so the restriction is not itself a post-treatment control) again yields no effect, though the confidence interval is wide given the smaller subsample. A control-window robustness (Appendix Figure~\ref{fig:window}) widens the comparison group from a youngest child aged exactly 5 up to 5--12 and finds the estimate stable throughout, so the upper end of the comparison group does not drive the result. The null is likewise insensitive to how sharply the statutory four-year boundary is drawn: reconstructing each child's age in months from the birth date and sliding the eligibility ceiling from 48 months (a strict ``until the fourth birthday'' reading, which reassigns the oldest treated children to the comparison group) up to the 60-month baseline leaves the estimate small and statistically insignificant at every cutoff (Appendix Table~\ref{tab:robustness}).

A related threat is that the treated and comparison groups differ in life stage and not only in eligibility: the treated group pools mothers of newborns, who face the most acute care demands and are the most likely to be on maternity leave, together with mothers of four-year-olds. Two further checks address this. A symmetric age donut around the five-year cutoff keeps only the oldest eligible children just below the boundary as treated and the youngest ineligible children just above it as the comparison group, matching the two groups tightly in the child's age. The estimate remains small and statistically insignificant as this window widens from twelve to twenty-four months. Splitting eligibility by the youngest child's age into bands of zero to one, two to three, and four years, each measured against the same 5--7 comparison group, yields a small and statistically insignificant estimate in every band, including for the youngest children, where the demand for telework is greatest and a pooled estimate might otherwise dilute an effect. Earnings tell the same story: measured in logs among workers with positive pay, the effect is small and statistically insignificant, and it is unchanged when earnings are winsorized at the 99th percentile.

\subsection{Inference, attrition, and the source of identification}\label{sec:inference}

Three further concerns bear on whether the null reflects inference or the panel rather than the policy. First, the reform varies nationally in time, so the effective number of independent policy shocks is far smaller than the millions of individual observations, and standard errors clustered on the household could understate uncertainty about a shock common to mothers of young children within a state. Appendix Table~\ref{tab:inference} re-runs the preferred specification clustering on the 27 states, with and without state-by-year-quarter fixed effects, and adds a wild cluster bootstrap over the states; because the individual fixed effects cannot be carried through the bootstrap, I partial them out by the within-transformation and bootstrap the residualized regression. The estimate is $-0.08$ points throughout and the bootstrap $p$-value is $0.82$, so the null survives few-cluster inference. The same table reports two alternative estimators the referees of the design would want: a repeated-cross-section difference-in-differences that replaces the individual fixed effects with child-age, maternal-age, and state-by-year-quarter fixed effects reproduces the $+0.35$-point cross-sectional selection of Section~\ref{sec:firststage}, and a local difference-in-discontinuities comparing children just below and just above the fifth-birthday boundary in months is small and statistically insignificant across bandwidths of twelve to twenty-four months. A pre-reform temporal placebo, assigning a fake reform in 2020Q2 using only pre-period data, is insignificant.

Second, because the preferred estimator relies on within-woman variation, non-random exit from the rotating panel could reshape the sample around the reform. Appendix Table~\ref{tab:attrition} shows it does not: the probability of reappearing in the next quarter is no different for young-child women after the reform (a differential-attrition coefficient of about $+0.1$ points, insignificant), and re-weighting each observation by the inverse of its predicted retention probability leaves the estimate small and statistically insignificant.

Third, Appendix Table~\ref{tab:identification} describes which women supply the within-person variation. With individual fixed effects and a common post date, $\beta$ is identified by within-woman variation in eligibility interacted with the post indicator. Among women observed on both sides of the reform, most retain a stable eligibility status; restricting the regression to these stable-status women produces a similar estimate, $-0.24$ percentage points, showing that the pooled null is not driven solely by births or children ageing across the cutoff. One caveat the design cannot resolve is a spillover: because the estimate compares eligible with ineligible mothers, it would net out any general-equilibrium effect operating on both, as could occur if granting priority to one worker reduced the remote positions available to another (a violation of the stable-unit-treatment-value assumption). The estimates should therefore be read as the effect on age-eligible mothers \emph{relative} to the comparison group, which need not equal the reform's total effect; given the null in levels, however, there is little relative effect to reallocate.

\subsection{A triple difference}\label{sec:triple}

Because Article 75-F is not gender-restricted, men with young children are themselves eligible, so they are not an untreated control and the triple difference does not estimate the policy's overall effect. What it tests is whether the young-child differential moved more for women than for men,
%
\begin{equation}
\text{DDD} = \big[\Delta Y^{W}_{0\text{--}4} - \Delta Y^{W}_{5\text{--}7}\big] - \big[\Delta Y^{M}_{0\text{--}4} - \Delta Y^{M}_{5\text{--}7}\big],
\label{eq:ddd}
\end{equation}
%
where $\Delta$ denotes the pre-to-post change in home-based work and the superscripts $W$ and $M$ index women and men. Appendix Table~\ref{tab:triple_diff} reports the effect for men and women separately together with this triple-difference coefficient: men show no effect, the women's estimate is the null already reported, and the interaction is small and insignificant, so I find no detectable female-specific response relative to eligible men. Appendix Table~\ref{tab:triple_diff_outcomes} extends the exercise to the other outcomes, where the significant coefficients track the pre-existing gender-differential trends discussed above rather than the reform.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}
% =============================================================================

The central finding is no detectable population-average increase in measured home-based work: the confidence interval excludes increases above approximately 0.5 percentage points, although the equivalence test at that margin is marginally inconclusive. The reform likewise induced no occupational sorting toward telework-eligible jobs. Its other potential margins divide by what the design can identify: earnings and hours among workers are small and insignificant conditional estimates but, because employment is potentially post-treatment, remain subject to compositional selection, while employment, participation, maternity leave, and the unconditional (extensive-margin-inclusive) earnings and hours measures fail the parallel-trends diagnostic, so I do not read any of them as effects of the reform in either direction. The results are consistent with a narrow statutory reach and limited implementation, but the PNADC does not separately identify worker awareness, employee requests, employer denials, or the supply of remote positions. The population the statute can reach is narrow: it binds only CLT employees (registered private-sector workers and the \emph{celetistas} of public companies and mixed-economy firms), yet a large share of Brazilian women work informally or outside formal employment, and among the formally employed only a minority hold jobs that can be done from home. These patterns are visible in Table~\ref{tab:descriptives}: fewer than three in ten treated women hold a formal job, and fewer than one in six are in a telework-eligible occupation. Table~\ref{tab:reach} traces the narrowing step by step: among women with a child aged 0--4, about one in five is a CLT employee and only about 6\% are CLT employees in a telework-eligible occupation who are not already working from home. This reach accounting is descriptive rather than causal: occupational teleworkability does not imply that an employer has an available remote position, but it shows how quickly the potentially affected population narrows. Home-based work is correspondingly rare (about 5\% of employed women in the sample), so even a mandate that bound perfectly among the teleworkable would move the population average little. Consistent with this, the effect is no larger among women already in telework-eligible occupations, and age-eligible mothers did not move toward those occupations to claim the benefit.

The reach of the statute is not merely narrow but narrows further at exactly the life-stage it targets. Motherhood pushes women out of formal, registered employment: across Latin America the birth of a first child produces a sharp and lasting rise in mothers' labor informality and a shift toward time-flexible but precarious arrangements (part-time work, self-employment, and unregistered jobs), while fathers' trajectories are unaffected \citep{berniell2021,berniell2023}. The mechanism is not only supply-side. In Brazil, formal employment carries mandated maternity protections, namely 120 days of paid leave and job stability from the confirmation of pregnancy until five months after the birth.\footnote{The 120-day paid maternity leave is guaranteed by Article~7, item~XVIII of the \href{https://www.planalto.gov.br/ccivil_03/constituicao/constituicao.htm}{1988 Federal Constitution}, and the job stability from the confirmation of pregnancy until five months after childbirth by Article~10, item~II(b) of the \href{https://www.planalto.gov.br/ccivil_03/constituicao/constituicao.htm\#adct}{Transitional Constitutional Provisions Act (ADCT)}.} The mandated-benefits literature has long shown that the cost of such protections falls on the wages and employment of the identifiable group that stands to use them, here women of childbearing age \citep{gruber1994,summers1989}. A mother who loses or leaves a registered job around a birth, or who is never hired into one, is beyond the reach of a priority that binds only CLT employees, whatever her demand for telework, so the very frictions that make a telework guarantee valuable also erode the formal-sector foothold the priority requires. This also cautions against reading the small reduced-form movements in employment and participation as effects of the reform: they track the pre-existing motherhood trajectory this literature documents and, as Section~\ref{sec:results} shows, fail the parallel-trends test, whereas home-based work, the one outcome with clean pre-trends, does not move.

Two further explanations are consistent with the null, though the data cannot separately establish either. First, workers are often unaware of workplace entitlements or face frictions in claiming them, and so may neither request nor act on them, the kind of incomplete take-up documented for other benefits \citep{bhargavamanoli2015,klevenkopczuk2011,finkelsteinnotowidigdo2019}; a priority that is never invoked imposes no cost on employers and prompts no reallocation. Second, where telework is feasible, employers who could offer it to a mother of a young child were largely already free to do so, so a non-binding legal preference adds little at the margin. The event study is consistent with a slow and, at most, modest adjustment: any movement appears only two to three years after enactment, once the measure had been converted into permanent law, rather than as a sharp break at the reform date.

Three limitations qualify the interpretation. First, the estimates are intent-to-treat effects of eligibility, not of telework itself, and are silent about the women who actually work from home; the effect of telework on them may well be larger, but it is not what the reform delivered, since the near-zero effect on home-based work means the reform moved almost no one into telework in the first place. Second, the sample runs through early 2026: the small, late upward drift in the event study could reflect a genuine slow-moving response that additional post-reform data would sharpen or dispel. Third, the sample is limited to prime-age women who head a household or are a head's partner (the group for whom a young child in the household reflects the woman's own caregiving), so the estimates need not extend to women in other household positions, such as adult daughters living with their parents. On the evidence available, however, the mandate did not move the outcome it targeted.

% =============================================================================
\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}
% =============================================================================

Brazil's 2022 reform gave mothers of young children a legal priority in the allocation of remote-work positions. Using the rotating panel of the national household survey and a difference-in-differences design built around the statutory age-4 threshold, I find that the reform did not raise the share of age-eligible women working from home. The population-average estimate is small and statistically insignificant, with a 95\% confidence interval of $[-0.67, 0.51]$ percentage points; it is robust to the fixed-effects specification, to the definition of treatment and controls, to the pandemic window, to clustering, and to the choice of comparison group; and it is accompanied by no occupational sorting toward telework-eligible jobs and no gendered pattern in a triple difference. Across subgroups the point estimates provide no evidence of an increase, although estimates for the CLT and teleworkable employees the law directly binds are too imprecise to establish equivalence. The result is a caution for a policy instrument that is cheap, increasingly popular, and easy to legislate: expanding access to workplace flexibility, and through it narrowing gender gaps, appears to require more than instructing employers to prefer one group over another. Without enforcement, or a larger stock of teleworkable positions to allocate, the priority remains one on paper.
