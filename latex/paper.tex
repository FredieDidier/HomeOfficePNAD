\begin{abstract}
\noindent In 2022, Brazil enacted a law requiring employers to give employees with children aged 4 or younger priority in the allocation of positions eligible for telework. Because the law grants a priority rather than an enforceable right, it need not translate into more telework in practice. Using quarterly rotating panel data from the national household survey for 2018 to 2026, and a difference-in-differences design that compares mothers of children aged 4 or younger with mothers of children aged 5 to 7, I find no effect on the share of women working from home. The null holds across specifications, subgroups, and robustness checks, and eligible mothers did not move into telework-friendly occupations. The evidence indicates that a discretionary, unenforceable priority did not change the outcome it targeted, a caution for a cheap and increasingly popular family-policy instrument.

\vspace{0.5em}
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} telework; work from home; gender; family policy; child penalty; Brazil

\vspace{0.3em}
\noindent\textbf{JEL codes:} J16; J22; K31
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% =============================================================================

The arrival and persistence of a young child remain one of the sharpest turning points in a woman's working life. Across high- and middle-income countries, mothers' earnings and employment fall abruptly at the birth of a first child and recover slowly, if at all, while fathers' trajectories are largely undisturbed \citep{kleven2019denmark,kleven2019countries}. In Brazil the tension between paid work and young children is severe: women's labor-force participation stands roughly 20 percentage points below men's, women perform close to twice as many weekly hours of unpaid domestic work and caregiving, and the employment rate of mothers with a child under 3 is well below that of women without young children \citep{ibge_gender}. At the same time, the pandemic made working from home a mainstream possibility for a meaningful minority of jobs and put workplace flexibility at the center of the policy debate on gender gaps \citep{barrero2023,dingelneiman2020}. Whether flexibility can be legislated into the hands of the workers who need it most is, therefore, a first-order question for population and family economics.

Economic theory offers a clear reason to expect flexibility to matter and an equally clear reason to doubt that a soft legal instrument can deliver it. On the demand side, \citet{goldin2014} argues that a large part of the remaining gender gap reflects the disproportionate reward that firms attach to long, inflexible hours, so that arrangements which relax the time constraint, telework prominent among them, are precisely what could narrow it. Workers, in turn, value such arrangements, and by the logic of compensating differentials \citep{rosen1986} will trade wages for them: \citet{maspallais2017} show that the average worker is willing to give up a non-trivial fraction of wages to avoid rigid schedules, and a growing body of causal evidence finds that home and hybrid work can raise, rather than lower, productivity and retention \citep{bloom2015,bloomhanliang2024}, even as access to remote work is shaped by selection as well as treatment \citep{emanuelharrington2024}. Yet the instrument at issue here is not a subsidy or an entitlement but a mandated priority, and the economics of mandated benefits warns that such mandates bind only weakly when compliance is costly and hard to verify \citep{summers1989,gruber1994}. When the law merely instructs employers to ``prioritize'' a group for a benefit that many jobs cannot supply, and provides no enforcement, standard incidence logic predicts little movement in equilibrium allocations.

This paper provides the first evidence on whether Brazil's telework-priority mandate changed the labor-market outcomes of the women it was meant to protect. In March 2022, through Provisional Measure 1108 and later Law 14.442, Brazil amended the Consolidation of Labor Laws (Article 75-F) to require employers to give priority in the allocation of telework-eligible positions to employees with children or legal dependents up to 4 years of age.\footnote{Provisional Measures have the force of law on publication; Provisional Measure 1108 took effect on 28 March 2022 and was converted, without change to Article 75-F, into Law 14.442 on 2 September 2022. See \href{https://www.planalto.gov.br/ccivil_03/_ato2019-2022/2022/mpv/mpv1108.htm}{Provisional Measure 1108/2022} and \href{https://www.planalto.gov.br/ccivil_03/_ato2019-2022/2022/lei/l14442.htm}{Law 14.442/2022}.} I evaluate the reform using the rotating panel of the Continuous National Household Sample Survey (PNAD Cont\'inua), which follows individuals for up to five consecutive quarters and, since 2018, records whether each worker performs her main job at home.

The design is a difference-in-differences framework around the reform, with individual fixed effects that absorb time-invariant selection into motherhood. It compares women whose youngest child is aged 4 or younger, who are eligible for the priority, with women whose youngest child is aged 5--7, who are similar in their selection into motherhood but just above the statutory threshold. The main outcome is whether a woman works from home. This is the compliance proxy, or first stage: it measures how much the mandate actually moved telework take-up among eligible women, and so bounds any downstream effect on earnings, hours, or fertility. The estimate is an intent-to-treat effect, since I observe legal eligibility rather than whether any employer granted telework; it therefore recovers the effect of being entitled to priority, the policy-relevant parameter.

I find no effect. The share of eligible women working from home did not rise relative to the comparison group after the reform: the difference-in-differences estimate is $-0.08$ percentage points on a pre-reform base of about 5\%, and at the 95\% level I can rule out increases larger than about 0.5 percentage points. An event study shows flat pre-reform trends, including through the 2020--2021 pandemic, and, at most, a small effect emerging only two to three years later. A placebo supports the design: mothers whose youngest child is 5--7, whom the law does not cover, show no change relative to women with no young children at all, so it is the age-4 threshold, and not a broader difference between mothers and other women, that identifies the effect.

Additional results show that the null does not mask offsetting responses. The effect on home office remains zero even among women who were already in telework-eligible occupations at baseline, the only women for whom a priority over telework positions could bind, and treated mothers did not move into telework-eligible occupations after the reform, so there is no ``priority-seeking'' occupational sorting. The effect is likewise indistinguishable from zero across subgroups: for formal and informal workers, for registered private-sector employees (the group the statute binds) and public employees, and across education, race, household structure (including single mothers), and age. The null survives alternative treatment and control definitions, dropping the pandemic years, state-by-year-quarter fixed effects, and alternative clustering. The same null appears when eligible and comparison mothers are matched narrowly on the child's age around the threshold, and even for the youngest children, for whom the demand for telework is greatest. It is also robust to a triple-differences design that adds men with young children as a third comparison group: because the statute is gender-neutral, these men are themselves eligible, so comparing women's response to theirs nets out any generic shock to parents of young children after 2022 and isolates the woman-specific response. The absence of an effect is not gendered.

The pattern points to an institutional explanation. A priority is not a right: it is only as valuable as the stock of teleworkable positions an employer has to allocate, and for most eligible women, who work informally, in the public sector, or in jobs that cannot be done from home, that stock is close to empty. That population narrows further at exactly the life stage the reform targets, because motherhood itself pushes women toward informal work. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} develops this reading and weighs it against alternatives.

The paper contributes to three literatures. The first is the economics of gender and workplace flexibility, in which telework, as an arrangement that relaxes the time constraint behind much of the gender gap, is a leading candidate to narrow it \citep{goldin2014,maspallais2017,bloom2015,flabbimoro2012} and whose prevalence the pandemic durably raised \citep{barrero2023,dingelneiman2020}. Against this backdrop, a recurring hope is that widening access to flexible arrangements can compress gender gaps \citep{bertrand2011,blaukahn2017}; the present evidence shows that access cannot be legislated into existence when the underlying jobs are not teleworkable and compliance is voluntary.

The second is the literature on the effectiveness of family policy and its relation to the child penalty \citep{kleven2019denmark,kleven2019countries,klevenatlas2025,addadustmann2017,olivettipetrongolo2017,angelov2016,lundborg2017}, which has largely evaluated leave, cash transfers, and childcare. Telework priority is a newer and far cheaper instrument that governments increasingly reach for,\footnote{Statutory rights to flexible or remote work for parents and carers have spread internationally. In the European Union, the \href{https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/dir/2019/1158/oj}{Work-Life Balance Directive (EU) 2019/1158} requires member states to grant workers with young children and caring responsibilities the right to request flexible working arrangements, including remote work; in the United States, jurisdictions such as San Francisco (the \href{https://www.sf.gov/information--family-friendly-workplace-ordinance}{Family Friendly Workplace Ordinance}) have adopted comparable right-to-request rules.} and evidence on whether it works is scarce; documenting that a well-identified version of it moved neither telework take-up nor labor-market outcomes is directly useful for the design of family and flexible-work policy.

The third is the economics of group-specific mandated benefits and their enforcement \citep{summers1989,gruber1994}, which has shown that a mandate targeted at an identifiable group can shift that group's wages or employment. Closest to the present study, \citet{battagliabrown2025} evaluate pregnancy-accommodation laws, another group-specific accommodation mandate aimed at women, with an analogous research design, a difference-in-differences reinforced by a triple difference that adds men as a comparison group, and find no effect on women's employment or wages. The present case reinforces and sharpens theirs: because I observe the compliance channel directly, I can show that the priority does not even raise telework take-up, so a benefit this weakly binding, and this poorly matched to the jobs the target group actually holds, moves nothing. It does so in a middle-income labor market with a large informal sector and limited enforcement \citep{ulyssea2018,gerardgonzaga2021,almeidacarneiro2012}, precisely the setting where soft mandates are most likely to fall flat.

The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section~\ref{sec:background} describes the reform, its institutional setting, and the theoretical impact of the priority, Section~\ref{sec:data} the data and sample, and Section~\ref{sec:strategy} the empirical strategy. Section~\ref{sec:results} presents the results: the first stage, the reduced-form outcomes, the mechanisms, the heterogeneity analysis, and the robustness checks and triple difference. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses the findings and Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

\section{Institutional background}\label{sec:background}
% =============================================================================

\subsection{The reform}

Brazilian labor law had no dedicated treatment of telework until 2017, when a broad labor reform added a chapter on remote work to the Consolidation of Labor Laws (CLT).\footnote{See \href{https://www.planalto.gov.br/ccivil_03/_ato2015-2018/2017/lei/l13467.htm}{Law 13.467/2017}, the 2017 labor reform, which added Chapter II-A (``Do Teletrabalho'') to the CLT.} The arrangement remained marginal until the pandemic forced a temporary but massive shift to home-based work in 2020--2021.\footnote{Home office covered about 5.8\% of the employed in 2019 (IBGE's PNAD Cont\'inua, reported by \href{https://www.cnnbrasil.com.br/economia/macroeconomia/trabalho-remoto-mais-que-dobrou-em-10-anos-mas-caiu-apos-pandemia-mostra-ibge/}{CNN Brasil}); it then jumped with the pandemic, reaching about 8.7 million workers at the May 2020 peak and averaging roughly 11\% of the employed over 2020 (IBGE's PNAD COVID-19 survey; see \href{https://www.ipea.gov.br/cartadeconjuntura/index.php/2021/02/o-trabalho-remoto-e-a-pandemia-o-que-a-pnad-covid-19-nos-mostrou/}{IPEA, \emph{Carta de Conjuntura}, 2021}).} As that shock receded, the federal government issued Provisional Measure 1108 on 25 March 2022 (published 28 March), which modernized the telework chapter and, in its Article 75-F, introduced the provision studied here;\footnote{The statute treats \emph{teletrabalho} and \emph{trabalho remoto} (telework and remote work) as synonyms (CLT, Art. 75-B, as amended), and I likewise use \emph{telework}, \emph{remote work}, and \emph{working from home} interchangeably throughout. The outcome is measured as performing one's main job at the worker's own residence (Section~\ref{sec:data}).} the measure was converted, with the article unchanged, into Law 14.442 on 2 September 2022.

Article 75-F directs employers to give priority, in allocating positions whose activities can be performed remotely, to employees with disabilities and to employees with children or legal dependents up to 4 years of age. Three features of the provision shape the analysis. First, it grants a priority in allocation, not a right to telework: an employer must place eligible employees ahead of others when distributing positions that are already teleworkable, but need not create such positions or grant remote work to anyone in particular. Second, it carries no dedicated enforcement mechanism or penalty and requires neither an employee request nor any certification. Third, it binds only on employees under the CLT (formal, registered, private-sector, and comparable workers) and not on the self-employed, on employers, or on the large population of workers without a registered contract. Together these features make Article 75-F a near-textbook soft mandate, and they motivate both the intent-to-treat reading of the estimates (Section~\ref{sec:strategy}) and the placebo splits by contract type (Section~\ref{sec:heterogeneity}).

\subsection{Theoretical impact of the priority on labor-market outcomes}\label{sec:framework}

A telework priority is a group-specific mandated benefit, and its potential effects can be organized around a single first stage. If the priority binds, eligible mothers move to the front of the queue for teleworkable positions, so its immediate, mechanical effect is to raise their probability of working from home. Every other outcome the reform could touch (earnings, hours, employment continuity, fertility) must operate through that channel: telework may let a mother stay employed through her child's early years, change her hours or her exposure to the motherhood earnings penalty, or shift the timing of births. The direction of these downstream effects is theoretically ambiguous \citep{goldin2014,olivettipetrongolo2017}, but their magnitude is bounded by the first stage, because an eligibility rule that does not change telework take-up cannot change outcomes that operate only through telework. This is why the analysis treats home office as the first stage and reads its estimate as an upper bound on any reduced-form effect.

Whether the priority binds at all is in doubt, for two reasons the mandated-benefits literature makes familiar. First, Article 75-F is an unusually soft mandate: it confers a priority rather than a right, attaches no penalty, and requires neither a request nor a certification, so an employer can comply by doing nothing and an eligible worker can hold the entitlement without ever invoking it. Mandates that protect an identifiable group can fail for exactly this reason: employment protection under the Americans with Disabilities Act, a similarly group-specific mandate, did not raise disabled workers' employment and by some estimates lowered it, because compliance was costly to enforce and easy to avoid \citep{acemogluangrist2001}, and this one sits near the same limiting case. Second, and specific to telework, a priority in allocating teleworkable positions is worth only as much as the stock of such positions an employer has to allocate; for the many eligible women who work informally, in the public sector, or in occupations that cannot be performed from home, that stock is close to empty, so the priority has nothing to act on.

These features yield the paper's testable structure. If the priority binds where it can, the first-stage effect on home office should be positive and concentrated among women in teleworkable occupations and under formal, private-sector contracts (the workers the statute reaches), and downstream outcomes should move in step. If instead the mandate is too soft or the teleworkable jobs too scarce, the first stage is zero everywhere, and so is everything downstream. The results below adjudicate between these cases.

\section{Data}\label{sec:data}
% =============================================================================

The analysis draws on the Continuous National Household Sample Survey (PNADC), the rotating quarterly household survey run by the Brazilian Institute of Geography and Statistics (IBGE) and the source of Brazil's official labor-market statistics. Each selected household is interviewed in up to five consecutive quarters, which yields a short individual-level panel. I use every quarter from the first quarter of 2018 (the first in which the survey records where each worker performs her main job, the variable behind the home-office outcome) through the first quarter of 2026. Individuals are linked across quarters from the survey's household and person identifiers together with an advanced matching procedure that reconstructs the panel even when interviews are incomplete; Appendix~\ref{app:pnadc} details the survey and the panel construction, and Appendix Table~\ref{tab:panel_retention} shows that the reconstructed panel captures genuine repeated observations of the same individuals and households.

The estimation sample is women aged 18--49 who are the household head or the head's spouse or partner, and whom the matching procedure links across quarters. The age range is the standard prime-age window for female labor supply: 18 marks entry into the adult labor market, and capping at 49 keeps the sample to working-age women and keeps the treated and comparison groups demographically comparable, rather than mixing in near-retirement women whose employment responds to different margins. Restricting to heads and spouses ensures that a young child in the household reflects the woman's own caregiving responsibility (for a child, stepchild, or grandchild in her care) rather than another household member's dependent. The main outcome is an indicator for working from home, defined as performing the main job at the worker's own residence. The remaining outcomes are real monthly labor earnings (in constant reais of the first quarter of 2026, the most recent quarter, deflated with IBGE's official PNADC deflator), usual weekly hours, indicators for employment and for labor-force participation, and an indicator for being on maternity leave, a proxy for a recent birth.\footnote{Earnings and usual hours are observed only for workers, so those two regressions condition on employment, a post-treatment variable. The conditioning is innocuous here because the reform moves neither the first stage nor employment (Section~\ref{sec:results}), leaving the composition of workers unchanged; the extensive margin (whether a woman works at all) is captured separately by the employment and labor-force-participation outcomes, which are defined over all sample women.} Fertility itself is not observed in the survey, so I do not estimate effects on childbearing; maternity leave enters only as a coarse proxy for a recent birth, and it is not interpreted causally (Section~\ref{sec:results}). The other derived variables (labor-force and employment status, contract type, education, and telework eligibility) are defined in Appendix~\ref{app:variables}. All statistics and estimates use the survey's sampling weights.

Treatment is defined by the presence in the household of a child aged 4 or younger, for a woman who is head or spouse. The primary comparison group is women whose youngest child is aged 5--7: mothers who are similar on the margins that select women into motherhood but whose youngest child has just crossed the statutory age-4 cutoff, so that comparing them with treated women isolates the effect of the threshold rather than of motherhood itself. A second comparison group, women with no child aged 0--7, serves two further purposes: it is larger, which sharpens precision, and it anchors a placebo. If mothers of children aged 5--7, whom the law does not cover, behave no differently from women with no young children at all, then the 5--7 group is a clean control and the design is not merely picking up a generic difference between mothers and other women; this is the placebo I report in Appendix Table~\ref{tab:robustness}. An occupation is classified as telework-eligible following the adaptation of \citet{dingelneiman2020} to Brazilian occupation codes by \citet{goes2020} and \citet{costa2024}, described in Appendix~\ref{app:telework}. A natural instinct would be to restrict the sample to the workers the priority can most plausibly reach: those in telework-eligible occupations, or the private-sector CLT employees that Article 75-F directly binds. Both margins, however, can respond to the reform itself (an eligible woman may move into a teleworkable occupation to claim the priority, or into or out of formal, registered employment), so conditioning the sample on either would mean controlling for a post-treatment outcome, a bad control \citep{angristpischke2009}. I therefore analyze the full sample of eligible women and use occupation and contract type only as baseline moderators, and occupation additionally as an outcome.

The statute covers children ``up to 4 years of age'', which means a child who has not yet reached the fifth birthday, so treatment is the presence of a child of completed age 0 to 4 and the comparison group begins at completed age 5. The survey records age in completed years, which places the youngest child in a one-year bracket but does not pin down its exact position relative to the four-year boundary: a completed-age-4 child is between four and five years old, and under a stricter reading of the statute (eligibility ending on the fourth rather than the fifth birthday) some of these children would belong in the comparison group. To show that the null does not depend on where this line is drawn, I reconstruct each child's age in months from the reported month and year of birth (defined in Appendix~\ref{app:variables}), anchored at the midpoint of the reference quarter since the survey does not release the exact interview date, and re-estimate the first stage while sliding the eligibility ceiling across the boundary, from a strict ``until the fourth birthday'' cutoff (48 months) up to the baseline ``until the fifth birthday'' definition (60 months). The estimate is a precise zero throughout (Section~\ref{sec:robustness}).

Table~\ref{tab:descriptives} reports summary statistics by group and period, and two patterns stand out. Working from home is uncommon in every group (about 5\% before the reform) and rises only modestly afterward, in parallel across groups; Figure~\ref{fig:trends} shows this quarter by quarter. Treated women are younger, participate less in the labor market, and are less likely to hold a formal job than the comparison groups. Identification does not require the groups to match on these levels: the individual fixed effects difference out time-invariant differences between them, and the design rests on parallel trends rather than on identical composition (Section~\ref{sec:strategy}).

\section{Empirical strategy}\label{sec:strategy}
% =============================================================================

I estimate the effect of eligibility with a two-way fixed-effects difference-in-differences model. For woman $i$ in year-quarter $t$,
%
\begin{equation}
y_{it} = \beta\,(\text{Treated}_{it}\times \text{Post}_t) + \gamma\,\text{Treated}_{it} + \alpha_i + \delta_t + \varepsilon_{it},
\label{eq:did}
\end{equation}
%
where $\text{Treated}_{it}$ equals one when woman $i$ has a child aged 4 or younger in quarter $t$, $\text{Post}_t$ equals one from the second quarter of 2022 onward, $\alpha_i$ are individual fixed effects, and $\delta_t$ are year-quarter fixed effects. The coefficient of interest is $\beta$. For the binary outcomes (home office and the employment, labor-force-participation, and maternity-leave indicators), Equation~\eqref{eq:did} is a linear probability model estimated by ordinary least squares, so $\beta$ is an effect on the probability of the outcome, reported throughout in percentage points. Because eligibility is time-varying, in that a woman is treated only in the quarters in which she has a young child, the contemporaneous term $\text{Treated}_{it}$ must be included: individual fixed effects do not absorb it, and omitting it would load the level ``motherhood penalty'' of women who transition into young-child status onto $\beta$. Standard errors are clustered at the household level and all regressions are weighted by the survey weights.\footnote{Because the reform date is common to all units rather than staggered across them, Equation~\eqref{eq:did} is a generalized two-way fixed-effects difference-in-differences that involves no comparisons between cohorts treated at different times, and so is not exposed to the negative-weighting problem that such comparisons can create in staggered designs \citep{goodmanbacon2021,dechaisemartin2020}. The only time variation in treatment is in eligibility itself, which switches on and off as a child crosses the age-4 threshold and is absorbed by the contemporaneous $\text{Treated}_{it}$ term. A single common post-period likewise makes the standard event study an adequate diagnostic without a heterogeneity-robust estimator.}

The identifying assumption is that, absent the reform, home-office rates for mothers of children aged 4 or younger and for the comparison group would have evolved in parallel. Individual fixed effects make this assumption considerably more credible than a cross-sectional comparison, because they remove all time-invariant differences between women who have a child aged 4 or younger and women whose youngest child is 5--7. The remaining threat, differential trends, is examined directly with an event study that traces the treated-versus-control gap quarter by quarter, normalized to the last pre-reform quarter.

The estimate recovers an intent-to-treat effect. The survey does not reveal whether a given employer reassigned a given woman to telework because of the law; it reveals eligibility and realized outcomes. Comparing eligible and non-eligible women around the reform therefore identifies the effect of being entitled to priority, regardless of take-up. As set out in Section~\ref{sec:framework}, the first-stage effect on home office is the compliance proxy, and its magnitude bounds any downstream effect.

\section{Results}\label{sec:results}
% =============================================================================

\subsection{The first stage: home office}

Table~\ref{tab:did_firststage} reports the effect on home office through a specification ladder. Column~1 is a raw regression with neither controls nor fixed effects; column~2 adds demographic controls; columns~3 and~4 add year-quarter fixed effects and then individual fixed effects; column~5 adds a quadratic in age. Across the first three columns the estimate is a marginally significant $+0.4$ percentage points, and neither observed demographics nor year-quarter fixed effects move it. Adding individual fixed effects (column~4) collapses it to $-0.08$ points and removes its significance, and adding age controls leaves it unchanged. The lesson is that the cross-sectional association reflects selection into young motherhood on time-invariant characteristics that observed demographics do not capture but individual fixed effects absorb, rather than an effect of the law; once that selection is netted out, no first stage remains. The preferred estimate (column~4), $-0.08$ percentage points, is small and tightly bounded: its 95\% confidence interval of roughly $[-0.67,\,0.51]$ excludes effects larger than about half a percentage point.

\paragraph{Event study.} Figure~\ref{fig:event_home} plots the estimated treated-versus-control gap in home office by quarter, for both comparison groups, normalized to the first quarter of 2022. The pre-reform coefficients are flat and close to zero throughout, including during the 2020--2021 pandemic, and a joint test does not reject that they are all zero (see the figure note), which supports the parallel-trends assumption. After the reform the gap remains near zero for roughly two years and drifts up to about one point in a few quarters of 2024--2025. This late movement does not survive aggregation: splitting the post-period into an early (2022--2023) and a late (2024--2026) window, in a single regression, gives effects of $-0.2$ and $+0.2$ percentage points, neither significant, and even the late window rules out increases larger than about 0.9 percentage points (Appendix Table~\ref{tab:robustness}). Whatever its source (the reform became permanent law only in September 2022, and telework arrangements are renegotiated slowly), the late drift leaves the pooled null intact.

\subsection{Reduced-form outcomes}

Table~\ref{tab:did_outcomes_A} reports the difference-in-differences estimates for the remaining outcomes using the primary comparison group. Real earnings, which enter in logs, and usual hours are statistically indistinguishable from zero, and precisely so: at the 95\% level I can rule out log-earnings effects larger than about 2\% and changes in usual weekly hours larger than about half an hour. Employment and labor-force participation show small positive coefficients of about 1 percentage point, both significant at the 5\% level, and maternity leave shows a coefficient of about $-0.9$ points, significant at the 1\% level. These coefficients cannot be attributed to the reform, for two reasons. First, with a null first stage, the law's only channel, telework access, did not move, so any change in these outcomes would have to operate through some other mechanism. Second, and more directly, home office is the only one of them whose pre-reform trends are flat: a joint test of the pre-reform event-study coefficients rejects parallel trends for employment ($\chi^2(16)=39.6$, $p<0.01$), labor-force participation ($\chi^2(16)=38.0$, $p<0.01$), and maternity leave ($\chi^2(16)=282$, $p<0.001$), but not for home office ($\chi^2(16)=12.6$, $p=0.70$). The significant employment, participation, and maternity-leave coefficients therefore reflect pre-existing differential trends between the groups rather than a break at the reform; the maternity-leave event study (Appendix Figure~\ref{fig:event_maternity}) shows this directly, with the treated-versus-control gap already declining well before 2022. Only home office, the outcome the analysis centers on, has clean pre-trends and can be read causally. Finally, the placebo that compares the two control groups (mothers of children 5--7 versus women with no young children, neither of whom the law touches) is $+0.04$ points and insignificant (Appendix Table~\ref{tab:robustness}), so the age threshold, rather than some generic difference between mothers and non-mothers, is what the design exploits.

\subsection{Mechanisms}\label{sec:mechanisms}

If the reform had worked through the telework-priority channel, its effect on home office should have concentrated among women whose jobs can actually be done from home, and it might have induced eligible women to move toward such jobs. Neither is present in the data.

Table~\ref{tab:mech_moderation} splits the first stage by whether a woman was in a telework-eligible occupation at baseline, measured in her first observed quarter and held fixed so that it is not itself an outcome. The effect is no different from zero among the eligible (the only women for whom a priority in allocating telework positions could bind) and among the non-eligible. Table~\ref{tab:mech_allocation} takes the natural next step and uses telework eligibility of the current occupation as the outcome. A reform that pushed mothers to seek telework-friendly jobs in order to claim the priority would produce a positive coefficient; the estimate is essentially zero for both comparison groups. Appendix Table~\ref{tab:occ_transition} makes the same point descriptively: among women observed both before and after the reform, the pattern of occupation switches into and out of telework eligibility is nearly identical for treated and control mothers. There is, in short, no behavioral response of occupational choice to the priority.

\subsection{Heterogeneity}\label{sec:heterogeneity}

Table~\ref{tab:heterogeneity} reports the first-stage effect across subgroups, all defined at baseline, and Appendix Figure~\ref{fig:hetero} summarizes the same estimates visually. The splits by contract type are the most informative, because the statute binds only on registered private-sector employees: if the estimate reflected Article 75-F rather than a generic shock to mothers of young children, it should appear for that group and vanish for informal and public-sector workers. A further split isolates single mothers, household heads with no co-resident partner. They are arguably the group with the strongest latent demand for the benefit: unable to share childcare with a partner, they place a premium on job flexibility and proximity, of which telework is the limiting case \citep{berniell2023,maspallais2017,lebarbanchon2021}. They are also the group for whom the employment friction created by mandated maternity protections is most salient \citep{gruber1994}. If the priority bound anywhere, it should bind for them.

Instead the estimate is indistinguishable from zero across the board: for formal and informal workers, for registered private-sector employees and for public employees, and across education, race, household structure, and age. Of the fifteen subgroups, two are individually significant, single mothers (about $-1.6$ percentage points) and women aged 40 to 49 (about $+1.4$), but with opposite signs, and the single-mother estimate is, if anything, wrong-signed: were the priority raising telework take-up among the women who most want it, its effect would be positive and largest precisely for single mothers, so a negative coefficient is the opposite of what a demand-driven take-up of the benefit would produce. That negative estimate is, moreover, not located where the statute could act. Decomposing the single-mother cell by contract type, at the foot of Table~\ref{tab:heterogeneity}, shows it is concentrated among single mothers outside the CLT, at about $-2.1$ percentage points, whom the law cannot reach, and is an insignificant $+0.2$ percentage points among the registered private-sector single mothers it does bind. Because this decomposition splits one already-listed cell rather than adding new subgroup hypotheses (the single-mother cell is mechanically the union of its two parts), it is reported as an interpretive channel check and held out of the multiple-testing family. A movement confined to the group the law cannot touch cannot be its effect.\footnote{The single-mother subgroup also shows flat pre-reform trends: a joint Wald test of the pre-reform event-study leads does not reject parallel trends ($\chi^2(16)=19.3$, $p=0.25$), so the negative coefficient is not a differential-trend artifact either; it is confined to the group the statute cannot bind and, as reported next, does not survive the correction for multiple testing.}

This is exactly what testing many subgroups invites: under the null, the more cuts one takes, the more likely that some estimate crosses a conventional threshold by chance, so a scattering of ``significant'' subgroups with inconsistent signs says little unless it survives a correction for the number of tests. It does not. Table~\ref{tab:heterogeneity} reports, for each subgroup, the raw $p$-value alongside a Holm step-down adjustment for multiple testing \citep{holm1979}: once the fifteen tests are accounted for, no subgroup is significant---the smallest adjusted $p$-value is $0.36$---and neither flagged cell is predicted by any theory. The heterogeneity analysis thus does not rescue a hidden effect; it shows the null is uniform, including among single mothers and where the law was supposed to bite hardest.

\subsection{Robustness}\label{sec:robustness}

Appendix Table~\ref{tab:robustness} collects robustness checks on the first stage. The null is unchanged when the post-period starts one quarter earlier, when the treatment definition excludes grandchildren, stepchildren, or both, when the pandemic years are dropped so that a clean 2018--2019 pre-period is compared with the post-reform quarters, and when state-by-year-quarter fixed effects absorb region-specific shocks. It is also unchanged under two-way clustering, which allows for correlation not only within a household but also across neighboring households in the same primary sampling unit, the census enumeration area that the survey draws within each geographic stratum.

Restricting the sample to women in a telework-eligible occupation at baseline (defined as in Table~\ref{tab:mech_moderation}, so the restriction is not itself a post-treatment control) again yields no effect, though the confidence interval is wide given the smaller subsample. A control-window robustness (Appendix Figure~\ref{fig:window}) widens the comparison group from a youngest child aged exactly 5 up to 5--12 and finds the estimate stable throughout, so the upper bound of the comparison group does not drive the result. The null is likewise insensitive to how sharply the statutory four-year boundary is drawn: reconstructing each child's age in months from the birth date and sliding the eligibility ceiling from 48 months (a strict ``until the fourth birthday'' reading, which reassigns the oldest treated children to the comparison group) up to the 60-month baseline leaves the first-stage estimate a precise zero at every cutoff (Appendix Table~\ref{tab:robustness}). A related threat is that the treated and comparison groups differ in life-stage and not only in eligibility, since the treated pool mothers of newborns, who face the most acute care demands and are the most likely to be on maternity leave, together with mothers of four-year-olds. Two further checks address this. Restricting the comparison to a symmetric age donut around the five-year cutoff, which keeps only the oldest eligible children just below the boundary against the youngest ineligible children just above it and so matches the two groups tightly in the child's age, leaves the first stage a precise zero as the window widens from twelve to twenty-four months. And splitting eligibility by the youngest child's age, zero to one, two to three, and four years, each measured against the same 5--7 comparison group, yields a precise zero in every band, including for the youngest children, where the demand for telework is greatest and where a pooled estimate might otherwise dilute an effect. Earnings tell the same story: measured in logs among workers with positive pay, the effect is a precise zero, and it is unchanged when earnings are winsorized at the 99th percentile.

Because Article 75-F is not gender-restricted, men with young children are also eligible; comparing eligible women with eligible men of the same age range (18--49) and household position (heads and spouses) allows a triple difference that nets out any generic shock to parents of young children after 2022 and isolates the woman-specific response. Table~\ref{tab:triple_diff} reports the first stage for men and women separately and the triple-difference coefficient. Men show no effect either; the women's estimate is the null already reported; and the triple-difference term is small and insignificant: the absence of an effect is not gendered. \section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}
% =============================================================================

The central finding is a precisely estimated null: a legal priority to telework for mothers of young children did not raise their telework take-up, and produced no detectable change in occupational sorting or labor-market outcomes. Of the two cases set out in Section~\ref{sec:framework}, the evidence points squarely to the second, in which the priority does not bind. The population it can reach is narrow: the statute binds only registered private-sector employees under the CLT, yet a large share of Brazilian women work informally or outside formal employment, and among the formally employed only a minority hold jobs that can be done from home. These patterns are visible in Table~\ref{tab:descriptives}: fewer than three in ten treated women hold a formal job, and fewer than one in six are in a telework-eligible occupation. Home office is correspondingly rare (about 5\% of employed women in the sample), so even a mandate that bound perfectly among the teleworkable would move the population average little. Consistent with this, the effect is no larger among women already in telework-eligible occupations, and eligible mothers did not move toward those occupations to claim the benefit.

The reach of the statute is not merely narrow but narrows further at exactly the life-stage it targets. Motherhood pushes women out of formal, registered employment: across Latin America the birth of a first child produces a sharp and lasting rise in mothers' labor informality and a shift toward time-flexible but precarious arrangements (part-time work, self-employment, and unregistered jobs), while fathers' trajectories are unaffected \citep{berniell2021,berniell2023}. The mechanism is not only supply-side. In Brazil, formal employment carries mandated maternity protections, namely 120 days of paid leave and job stability from the confirmation of pregnancy until five months after the birth. The mandated-benefits literature has long shown that the cost of such protections falls on the wages and employment of the identifiable group that stands to use them, here women of childbearing age \citep{gruber1994,summers1989}. A mother who loses or leaves a registered job around a birth, or who is never hired into one, is beyond the reach of a priority that binds only CLT employees, whatever her demand for telework, so the very frictions that make a telework guarantee valuable also erode the formal-sector foothold the priority requires. This also cautions against reading the small reduced-form movements in employment and participation as effects of the reform: they track the pre-existing motherhood trajectory this literature documents and, as Section~\ref{sec:results} shows, fail the parallel-trends test, whereas home office, the one outcome with clean pre-trends, does not move.

Two further explanations likely reinforce the null. First, workers are often unaware of workplace entitlements or face frictions in claiming them, and so may neither request nor act on them, the kind of incomplete take-up documented for other benefits \citep{bhargavamanoli2015,klevenkopczuk2011,finkelsteinnotowidigdo2019}; a priority that is never invoked imposes no cost on employers and prompts no reallocation. Second, where telework is feasible, employers who could offer it to a mother of a young child were largely already free to do so, so a non-binding legal preference adds little at the margin. The event study is consistent with a slow and, at most, modest adjustment: any movement appears only two to three years after enactment, once the measure had been converted into permanent law, rather than as a sharp break at the reform date.

Three limitations qualify the interpretation. First, the estimates are intent-to-treat effects of eligibility, not of telework itself, and are silent about the women who actually work from home; the effect of telework on them may well be larger, but it is not what the reform delivered, since the near-zero first stage means the reform moved almost no one into telework in the first place. Second, the sample runs through early 2026: the small, late upward drift in the event study could reflect a genuine slow-moving response that additional post-reform data would sharpen or dispel. Third, the sample is limited to prime-age women who head a household or are a head's partner (the group for whom a young child in the household reflects the woman's own caregiving), so the estimates need not extend to women in other household positions, such as adult daughters living with their parents. On the evidence available, however, the mandate did not move the outcome it targeted.

% =============================================================================
\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}
% =============================================================================

Brazil's 2022 reform gave mothers of young children a legal priority in the allocation of remote-work positions. Using the rotating panel of the national household survey and a difference-in-differences design built around the statutory age-4 threshold, I find that the reform did not raise the share of eligible women working from home. The estimate is a precise zero; it is robust to the fixed-effects specification, to the definition of treatment and controls, to the pandemic window, to clustering, and to the choice of comparison group; it is uniform across subgroups, including the registered private-sector employees the law binds; and it is accompanied by no occupational sorting toward telework-eligible jobs and no gendered pattern in a triple difference. The result is a caution for a policy instrument that is cheap, increasingly popular, and easy to legislate: expanding access to workplace flexibility, and through it narrowing gender gaps, appears to require more than instructing employers to prefer one group over another. Without enforcement, or a larger stock of teleworkable positions to allocate, the priority remains one on paper.
